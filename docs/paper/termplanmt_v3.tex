\begin{abstract}
Terminology consistency is a patient-safety requirement in regulatory medical
translation, yet neural MT systems are structurally indifferent to
cross-segment term stability. We present \textbf{TermPlanMT}, a six-system
ablation study on Section~4.8 of the KEYTRUDA SmPC---a 126-segment
French--English corpus with maximum adverse-event density. Our central finding
is that Concept Coverage Rate (CCR), the fraction of source spans grounded in
a MedDRA ontology graph, is the binding constraint on terminology-aware MT: at
39\% CCR graph-free baselines (S1--S2) outperform all graph-augmented systems
on BLEU and chrF, while improving NER quality yields $\approx$7~BLEU gains for
Mistral graph systems (S3--S5-Mistral) but not for graph-free S1 or S5-NLLB;
S2 is condition-invariant (same inference run in both conditions); S3--S5-Mistral are not (Table~\ref{tab:delta}). We
further show that our Hierarchy-Aware Terminology Metric (HTM) scores the
certified human reference at about 0.15--0.18 depending on NER manifest, a
score unchanged by full MedDRA ancestor alias expansion, confirming a
structural mismatch between MedDRA nomenclature and professional translation
practice. Finally, ambiguous MedDRA grounding causes the planning stage to
abstain from locking critical terms, explaining a paradox in which
graph-augmented systems produce \emph{lower} cross-document consistency than
graph-free baselines. We complement aggregate scores with a glossary-oracle
ablation (S6) and segment-level qualitative analysis (missing-term rates,
drift, modal agreement) exported under \texttt{error\_analysis/} in the
project repository.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

EMA Summaries of Product Characteristics (SmPCs) require a single
adverse-event concept to receive one identical English rendering across an
entire document---a requirement that affects pharmacovigilance signal detection
and patient safety \citep{neves2022evaluation}. Neural MT handles medical prose
fluently but is structurally indifferent to cross-segment consistency,
producing term drift (same source term, multiple renderings), concept
flattening (LLT-level terms collapsed to hypernyms), and coverage gaps
(multi-token spans outside NER). MedDRA's five-level hierarchy
(Table~\ref{tab:meddra}) provides validated English labels that are an
attractive grounding resource---if the grounding pipeline can cover enough of
the source text.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1)~A six-system ablation ladder (\S\ref{sec:systems}) isolating the effects
of NER recall, ontology grounding, and decoding enforcement.
(2)~HTM and alias-expanded HTM-A (\S\ref{sec:metrics}), with a diagnostic
evaluation showing the metric systematically penalises the human reference.
(3)~A CCR-bottleneck experiment (\S\ref{sec:results}) showing that NER
quality---not graph retrieval strategy---determines whether ontology grounding
helps.
(4)~A mechanistic account (\S\ref{sec:consistency}) of why graph-augmented
systems are \emph{less} consistent than graph-free ones.
(5)~Structured qualitative error analysis tying drift to ambiguous grounding
and abstaining locks, with reproducible CSV/MD artefacts.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Lexically constrained decoding \citep{hokamp2017lexically,post2018fast} and
terminology-aware training \citep{dinu2019training} enforce surface-level term
appearance but ignore ontological hierarchy and do not address candidate
selection when multiple levels are valid. \citet{herold2023improving} show
that extending NMT context windows can degrade fluency, motivating document-level
LLMs as a consistency baseline. \citet{labrak2024biomistral} release
BioMistral, the medically adapted LLM used for NER here.
\citet{neves2022evaluation} identify adverse-event terminology as a
high-priority MT failure mode. GraphRAG-style retrieval
\citep{edge2024local} into LLM prompts provides the template for our S3
system, adapting knowledge-grounded MT \citep{moussallem2019augmenting} to
a Neo4j MedDRA backend.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:data}

\paragraph{Corpus.}
Section~4.8 (Undesirable Effects) of the KEYTRUDA (pembrolizumab) SmPC
\citep{ema2024keytruda}: 126 aligned French--English segment pairs after
excluding segment~48\_028 (a dense frequency table whose n-gram overlap
distorts corpus metrics). Coverage: $\approx$4,400 words; all three failure
modes at high density.

\caption{MedDRA hierarchy. Adverse-event mentions in clinical

\label{tab:meddra}
\end{table}

\paragraph{NER conditions as a CCR perturbation.}
Rather than comparing two NER systems, we use them as a controlled perturbation
of CCR within the same pipeline. \textbf{Baseline NER} (\texttt{ner\_biollm}):
BioMistral-7B zero-shot; CCR~=~39.0\%, 184 grounded spans across 472 extracted
(126-segment evaluation set). This condition exhibits a systematic
artefact---the model generates \textit{hypothyro\"{i}die} for 92/127 segments
regardless of content---partially contaminating consistency measurements.
\textbf{Fine-tuned (higher-quality) NER} (\texttt{ner\_biollm\_ft}):
BioMistral-7B fine-tuned on a hierarchical MedDRA SFT dataset; 211 grounded
spans across 580 extracted (36.4\% CCR) with cleaner boundaries and no
hallucinated terms. Graph-augmented systems benefit substantially under this
condition, confirming that \emph{effective} span quality (clean boundaries,
usable grounding)---not raw graph retrieval---is the binding constraint once
contamination is controlled.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:systems}

\paragraph{Terminology planning.}
Before translation, \texttt{compute\_global\_locks()} scores each candidate
English rendering~$c$:
\begin{equation}
\text{Score}(c) = \alpha\,\text{Ctx}(c) + \beta\,\text{Nbr}(c)
               - \gamma\,\text{HP}(c)
\end{equation}
where Ctx is cosine similarity to source segments containing the term,
Nbr is similarity to adjacent MedDRA concept renderings, and
$\text{HP} = |\hat{d} - d_{\text{src}}|$ penalises wrong specificity level
($\alpha{=}0.5$, $\beta{=}0.3$, $\gamma{=}0.2$). When a term matches
\emph{multiple} MedDRA concepts, no lock is committed
(\S\ref{sec:consistency}).

\section{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\paragraph{Fluency.} Corpus BLEU \citep{papineni2002bleu} and
chrF\texttt{++} \citep{popovic2017chrf} via SacreBLEU against one
professional English reference.

\paragraph{HTM and HTM-A.}
For each segment containing a grounded French span, the hypothesis is scored
against the MedDRA graph: $s_i = 1.0$ (exact level match), $0.5$ (same
branch, wrong specificity), or $0.0$ (different branch / absent).
$\text{HTM} = \text{mean}(s_i)$. HTM-A relaxes matching across three alias
tiers: T1 exact LLT; T2 adds PT parent; T3 adds all same-branch ancestors.
We compute \textbf{rHTM} by applying HTM to the professional reference as
hypothesis, establishing a performance ceiling.

\paragraph{Hyp--Ref Agreement (HRA).}
Fraction of HTM-measured segments where the system's terminology choice
matches the professional reference---a measure of human-like translation
independent of MedDRA correctness.

\paragraph{CCR and drift.}
CCR~$=$~grounded/extracted spans; identical across S1--S6 for a given NER
condition. Term drift~$= (D-1)/(N-1)$ for $D$ distinct renderings across
$N$ segments; drift~$=0$ is perfect consistency.

\begin{finding}
S1 is unchanged (no graph). S2 is exactly zero: the same inference JSONL is
shared across both NER conditions, so $\Delta$BLEU is zero by construction.
S3--S5-Mistral show $\approx$7-point $\Delta$BLEU with intervals strictly
above zero, isolating the NER bottleneck on the graph path. S5-NLLB is
effectively flat. The S6 oracle glossary yields negligible gain, showing
that logit boosting with reference-extracted terms does not improve over
the graph path once NER quality is matched.
\end{finding}

Graph-free systems (S1, S5-NLLB) dominate on corpus BLEU and chrF in the
baseline NER condition (Table~\ref{tab:biollm}) because the graph influences
fewer than 40\% of adverse-event spans while injected MedDRA strings still hurt
fluency where the graph is active. The contrast between zero $\Delta$BLEU for S1, S2, and S5-NLLB
versus large $\Delta$BLEU for
S3--S5-Mistral in Table~\ref{tab:delta} isolates the bottleneck upstream of
graph retrieval on the Mistral-augmented branch. Higher span coverage with
cleaner NER would likely reverse the S2/S3 ranking.

\subsection{Missing Terms, Flattening, and Consistency}

Under baseline NER, S5-Mistral achieves the lowest missing-term rate; S1-NLLB
misses the majority of grounded terms. Graph systems benefit from cleaner NER:
S3's missing-term rate falls substantially between conditions. Zero concept
flattening events are observed (all grounded L5 source terms produce L5
hypotheses), partly an evaluation artefact---HTM cannot detect flattening
unless upper-level aliases are provided, a limitation HTM-A addresses.

Mean cross-document modal agreement, averaged over French terms with $\geq$2
segments in \texttt{error\_analysis/}, is highest for graph-free systems under
baseline NER (S1: 0.993; S2: 0.984) while S3 scores 0.946; under fine-tuned
NER, S3 rises to 0.976, surpassing S1 (0.970) but remaining below S2
(0.985)---so the ``graph hurts consistency'' pattern is clearest under noisy
baseline NER and partially attenuates when NER improves, matching the
missing-term and BLEU deltas.

\section{The HTM--Practice Gap}
\label{sec:htm_gap}

\subsection{Why the Reference Scores Near Zero}

On the baseline NER manifest, rHTM~$= 0.151$ on the professional reference
(Table~\ref{tab:biollm}); under fine-tuned NER the reference HTM is 0.178
(Table~\ref{tab:finetuned}) because the grounded span set differs.
In both cases the score stays below several MT systems on HTM. Professional
translators do not transcribe MedDRA LLT strings verbatim: the LLT
\textit{Immune-mediated pneumonitis} appears as \textit{pneumonitis} in
tabular contexts and as \textit{immune-related pneumonitis} in descriptive
contexts.

\subsection{Alias Expansion Does Not Close the Gap}

\begin{finding}
The ancestor chain for \textit{pneumopathie inflammatoire} contains
\textit{pneumonitis}, so the null T1$\to$T3 delta is not a graph coverage
gap. Professional translators use phrasings such as \textit{immune-related
pneumonitis} and \textit{inflammatory lung disease} that lie entirely outside
the MedDRA preferred-label tree at any level. The mismatch is structural:
MedDRA labels are designed for pharmacovigilance coding, not regulatory prose.
\end{finding}

HTM should therefore be interpreted as a measure of MedDRA-proximity, not
translation quality. The HRA column is the complementary measure. Notably,
graph-augmented systems score consistently \emph{lower} on HRA than S2
(Table~\ref{tab:biollm}: S3~=~0.601 vs.\ S2~=~0.699;
Table~\ref{tab:finetuned}: S3~=~0.440 vs.\ S2~=~0.476): the graph pushes
outputs toward MedDRA labels that professional translators avoid.

\section{Consistency Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:consistency}

\paragraph{Term drift (qualitative).}
\textit{Pneumopathie inflammatoire} occurs in four baseline segments where it
is grounded for S3/S4/S5-Mistral, and in eight fine-tuned segments (more NER
hits for the same French string). Those systems emit two renderings---often
\textit{pneumonitis} in incidence-style sentences and \textit{immune-mediated
pneumonitis} elsewhere---yielding drift scores of $1/3$ (baseline) and $1/7$
(fine-tuned). S1 collapses to one rendering for this span in both conditions
(drift~0). S2 is stable in baseline (drift~0) but shows non-zero drift under
fine-tuned NER once the span appears in additional contexts. S3/S4/S5-Mistral
thus exhibit the clearest cross-segment inconsistency for this exemplar.

Mean modal agreement (\S\ref{sec:results}) shows the same pattern at corpus
level: graph-free systems rank highest under baseline NER; graph-augmented
systems recover partially under fine-tuned NER but do not overtake S2.

\paragraph{Mechanism.}
\textit{Pneumopathie inflammatoire} maps to two distinct MedDRA concepts
in the string cache (one of 7,679 ambiguous normalised keys). The planning
stage sets \texttt{has\_planning\_lock~=~0} for any ambiguous term and
commits no lock. S3/S4/S5-Mistral then resolve the ambiguity context-locally
per segment from RAG context: incidence-focused segments attract
\textit{pneumonitis}; mechanism-focused segments attract
\textit{immune-mediated pneumonitis}. S1-NLLB produces zero drift not
because it reasons about terminology, but because its fixed vocabulary
has no mechanism for context-dependent selection.

\begin{finding}
This failure is independent of CCR. Even with perfect NER recall, any term
matching multiple MedDRA concepts will be left unlocked, producing potential
drift in all Mistral-based graph systems. The fix is \emph{soft ambiguity
resolution}: commit the top-scoring candidate as a provisional lock flagged
for human review, consistent with CAT-tool workflows.
\end{finding}

\paragraph{Output contamination.}
In the fine-tuned NER condition, 18/127 segments per system (14.2\%) contain
prompt contamination: Mistral reproduced MedDRA metadata strings and
instruction markers from its own system prompt. These rows were replaced
with a null hypothesis for metric computation. Contamination biases raw
scores in opposite directions---deflating BLEU (precision destroyed by
injected strings) while inflating chrF (character overlap increased)---
confirmed by S3 raw vs.\ filtered: BLEU 15.36$\to$19.58, chrF
37.20$\to$35.33. S1 and S2 are unaffected as they receive no graph
context in their prompts.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results collectively show that the TermPlanMT pipeline is architecturally
sound but CCR-limited. Graph-augmented systems improve substantially when
supplied with cleaner, higher-coverage NER input. The absence of BLEU
movement for S1 (and near-zero change for S5-NLLB), together with large
Mistral-graph $\Delta$BLEU in Table~\ref{tab:delta}, is direct evidence that
the bottleneck lies upstream of graph retrieval on the graph-augmented path.

The S6 oracle glossary (terms extracted from the reference via an LLM) yields
only marginal BLEU improvement over S5-NLLB (+0.09 points), suggesting that
the quality of the terminology source matters less for NLLB decoding than the
NER coverage upstream.

The HRA--HTM inversion---graph systems score higher on HTM yet lower on
HRA---reveals a real tension: optimising toward MedDRA formal labels
diverges from how certified translators actually write. For regulatory MT
in practice, HRA is the more meaningful target.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\textit{Single document and reference.} All results derive from one SmPC
section against one professional translation, limiting generalisability.
\textit{HTM ceiling.} rHTM is low in absolute terms (0.151--0.178 across
NER manifests) and invariant to alias expansion within a manifest, so HTM
is only interpretable relatively.
\textit{NER artefact.} The baseline NER condition contains hallucinated spans
(\textit{hypothyro\"{i}die} in 92/127 segments).
\textit{Contamination.} Fine-tuned-condition Mistral-based graph systems exhibit
14.2\% prompt contamination; filtered scores are used throughout.
\textit{S6 oracle.} The glossary is extracted from reference translations via
Ollama; genuine oracle quality depends on extraction recall.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

TermPlanMT demonstrates that CCR is the binding constraint on
graph-augmented regulatory MT: at low coverage graph-free baselines (S1,
S5-NLLB) dominate on BLEU and chrF, while improving NER quality yields
$\approx$7-point corpus BLEU gains for Mistral graph systems with bootstrap
intervals excluding zero. Three broader findings emerge: MedDRA nomenclature
and professional translation practice are structurally misaligned in ways that
alias expansion cannot fix; ambiguous grounding produces predictable term
drift through a mechanism fully explained by the planning stage's
abstain-on-ambiguity policy; and consistency metrics must control for system
type, since NLLB's apparent consistency is an artefact of its fixed
vocabulary. The glossary oracle (S6) shows that replacing the MedDRA graph
with reference-extracted terms provides negligible benefit, pointing to NER
coverage rather than term-source quality as the primary bottleneck.
Substantially higher CCR with cleaner span boundaries would likely be required
to reverse the S2/S3 fluency ordering; quantifying that threshold is left to
future work with broader French biomedical NER.
